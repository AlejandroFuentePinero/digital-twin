# Long-Term Changes in Populations of Rainforest Birds in the Australia Wet Tropics Bioregion: A Climate-Driven Biodiversity Emergency

**Source:** https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0254307
**Published:** 2021 in *PLOS One* (co-authored).

## What it is

A co-authored *PLOS One* paper using **17 years of bird monitoring** (2000–2016) across 114 sites spanning sea level to 1,500 m elevation in the Australian Wet Tropics, documenting that most mid- and high-elevation species lost >40% of abundance at their lower elevational edges, while lowland species expanded upward by up to 190%. Upland endemics declined by almost 50%.

The paper provides empirical evidence that the **Outstanding Universal Value of the Wet Tropics World Heritage Area is rapidly degrading** under climate change — a finding with direct conservation-policy weight beyond ecological interest.

## Methods

The dataset is the substantive technical contribution: a 17-year, 114-site, 47-species spatiotemporal monitoring record across a complete elevational gradient is rare globally and required substantial design and field-effort investment.

Analytical pipeline:

- **Long-term monitoring data** structured by site, year, elevation, and species — enabling per-species trend extraction across the gradient rather than aggregate metrics.
- **Species-level abundance trend analysis** comparing observed counts at the lower vs upper elevational edges of each species' range over the 17-year window.
- **Cross-species pattern aggregation** by elevational specialist class (lowland, mid-elevation, upland) to surface community-level shifts.
- **Comparison to expected patterns under thermal range tracking** — the quantified abundance shifts at range edges are interpreted against the range-shift expectation generated by the elevational climate gradient.

**Data:** 17-year monitoring record (2000–2016), 114 sites spanning sea level to 1,500 m, 47 bird species; spatiotemporal climate covariates.

## Theoretical contribution

The paper's contribution rests on the **dataset design** as much as on individual analytical choices:

- A 17-year, 114-site, 47-species monitoring dataset across a complete elevational gradient is one of very few comparable datasets globally — most equivalent studies are shorter or sample a single elevation. Field-based long-term elevational monitoring at this density takes years to build and was made possible by the established Centre for Tropical Biodiversity & Climate Change monitoring infrastructure.
- The empirical finding (asymmetric range-edge contraction at low elevations + upward expansion of lowland species) is the **observational evidence** for the *escalator-to-extinction* mechanism that the *DDI 2022 vertebrate communities* paper later quantifies through dispersal simulation. The two papers are complementary: empirical pattern + mechanistic explanation.
- Provides decision-grade evidence for **World Heritage Area management** — the Wet Tropics is a UNESCO site whose Outstanding Universal Value is under direct climate threat.

## Results

- **Most mid and high elevation species lost >40% abundance** at their lower elevational edges over the 17-year window.
- **Lowland species expanded by up to 190%** upward over the same period — asymmetric upward redistribution.
- **Upland endemics declined by almost 50%** overall.
- The Wet Tropics World Heritage Area's Outstanding Universal Value is documented as rapidly degrading under climate change.

The findings have been used in subsequent threatened-species nominations and conservation-status assessments for endemic Wet Tropics rainforest birds.
