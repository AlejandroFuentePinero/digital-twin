# Research Overview

Alejandro's academic research spans two major phases: a PhD investigating climate change impacts on tropical montane biodiversity (2020–2024), and postdoctoral work on heat mortality risk in spectacled flying foxes (2024–2026). Both are grounded in the same methodology: Bayesian hierarchical modelling, long-term monitoring data, and mechanistic inference that produces evidence with real conservation consequences.

---

## Postdoctoral Research: Spectacled Flying Foxes (2024–2026)

**Institution:** James Cook University, Cairns, Australia
**Focus:** Heat-driven mortality risk in endangered spectacled flying foxes (*Pteropus conspicillatus*)

The spectacled flying fox is an endangered megabat critical for pollination and seed dispersal in northeast Australian rainforests. A 2018 heatwave killed approximately one-third of the entire population in urban Cairns, making this a high-stakes conservation problem.

**What the research does:**
- Investigates thermal environments of flying fox roosts across their range (Wet Tropics to Cape Tribulation)
- Integrates ecological monitoring, physiological studies, and forecasting models
- Develops a predictive tool to identify high-risk heat events and pinpoint vulnerable roost sites
- Translates findings into actionable guidance for conservation managers (misting systems, roost exclusion, targeted revegetation)

**Scale:** ~15,000 records across 28 years and 500+ locations.

**A student project supervised** (Olivia Bond, Johns Hopkins/SIT 2025) found that solar exposure — not just temperature alone — is a critical and overlooked driver of thermoregulatory stress in flying foxes, with the most extreme behavioural responses occurring when high temperature coincided with high solar exposure.

## PhD Research: Climate Change Impacts on Tropical Montane Biodiversity (2020–2024)

**Institution:** James Cook University | **Award:** Cum Laude
**Focus:** How climate change drives population declines in the Australian Wet Tropics, using rainforest birds and ringtail possums as study systems

The Australian Wet Tropics is one of the world's most irreplaceable biodiversity hotspots. Alejandro's PhD drew on 30 years of region-wide monitoring data across 150+ locations to unravel the mechanisms linking climate change to population collapse.

**Key contributions:**

**1. Population trends of rainforest birds** — using hierarchical spatiotemporal models on 17–30 years of monitoring data (47–114 species), showed that warming has driven upland specialists into rapid decline (>40% loss at lower elevational edges) while lowland species expanded upward. Documented a clear "escalator to extinction" pattern. Results contributed to threatened species nominations for 15 rainforest species.

**2. Abundance–suitability relationships** — showed that species distribution models (SDMs) can predict fine-scale local abundance with high accuracy (average 55% deviance explained), providing a powerful tool for conservation planning from occurrence data alone.

**3. Community reshuffling** — simulated climate-induced uphill dispersal of 7,613 community assemblages for all vertebrate species across the elevational gradient. Heterogeneous dispersal rates among species produced marked community disassembly, with particularly severe local extinction rates at high elevations.

**4. Possum population viability forecasting** — using Bayesian hierarchical open population models on 30 years of monitoring (1992–2021), forecasted population collapse for rainforest ringtail possums (*Pseudochirops archeri*, *Hemibelideus lemuroides*) by 2050 under increasing warming and heatwave frequency. Results supported threatened species re-listing.

**5. Mechanistic biophysical framework** — integrated biophysical, nutritional, and population models to show how temperature stress and foraging limitations drive species-specific population declines. A novel broadly applicable mechanistic framework bridging "bottom-up" biophysical models with traditional "top-down" statistical approaches.

**6. Mountains as natural laboratories** — argued (with co-authors) that the sharp climatic contrasts of mountain systems make them ideal natural experiments for discovering the mechanisms underlying biological change at a global scale (Nature Climate Change, 2026).

**7. Altitudinal migration and community breathing** — using hierarchical Bayesian N-mixture models on 16 years of monitoring (2000–2016, 100+ species, 100+ sites across the full elevational gradient), quantified partial altitudinal migration as a system-wide process. Defined migration as the season × elevation interaction in a joint abundance-detection model. Key finding: a clear community-level seasonal "breathing" — total bird abundance peaks in lowlands in winter and uplands in summer. Establishes a generalisable Bayesian workflow for detecting redistribution in long-term imperfect-detection datasets. (Under review, *Diversity and Distributions*)

## A key conceptual finding: press vs pulse climate effects

A through-line across the PhD: different species and systems respond to different *types* of climate change, not just different magnitudes. **Press effects** are gradual, directional changes (steady warming, shifted rainfall patterns) — rainforest birds respond primarily to these, showing slow but sustained population shifts across elevational gradients. **Pulse effects** are extreme discrete events (heatwaves, cyclones) — ringtail possums respond primarily to these, with population collapses following individual extreme heatwave years. Understanding whether a system is press- or pulse-driven determines the appropriate monitoring strategy, model structure, and conservation intervention. This distinction runs through the 2022 and 2025 GCB and DDI papers and was synthesised in the PhD completion seminar (JCU, 2024).

## PhD Research Approach

**Data:** multi-decadal ecological monitoring data (birds, marsupials), climate data (temperature, precipitation, heatwaves, cyclones, droughts), physiological and nutritional data (foliage chemistry, isotopes, soil nutrients), biophysical data (microclimate, radiation).

**Methods:** Bayesian hierarchical models, N-mixture models, open population models, integrated population models, species distribution models (SDMs), spatiotemporal forecasting, biophysical models, landscape connectivity analysis.

**Tools:** R, JAGS/WinBUGS, GIS/remote sensing, reproducible scripting pipelines.

**Output:** 4 first-author papers in Global Change Biology, Diversity & Distributions (×2), Ecography, plus contributions to Oecologia, Nature Climate Change, PLOS One. Conservation listings for 15 rainforest species.
